A crowdsourced blacklist identified 176 Chinese companies running 996 schedules. The counter-list of companies following labor law had 34 firms. Twenty-seven of them were branches of foreign companies.

Huawei's founder Ren Zhengfei distributed folding mattresses to new hires in the 1990s. They slept at headquarters on orders they couldn't lose. When a senior exec said he needed to relocate to Beijing for his family, Ren reportedly said: "Get a divorce."

That mattress was a frame - a story that settles what's reasonable before anyone argues. 996's frame: overwork is proof you deserve to exist.

Huawei's "Striver Declaration" asked staff to choose: "striver" or "labourer." Strivers gave up paid leave and overtime pay. Labourers kept benefits but killed their prospects.

Nobody was forced. Everyone chose. And the bargain was real; Ren kept only 1.4% of Huawei. Staff held the rest through stock ownership.

Jack Ma called 996 "a huge blessing." JD[dot]com's Richard Liu: "slackers are not my brothers." The stakes are simple. If 996 is devotion, the founder is a visionary. If it's suffering, he's an exploiter. Everything rides on whether the frame holds.

Then the frame said the quiet part out loud. A 22-year-old Pinduoduo employee collapsed at 1:30 AM walking home from work and died. The company's Zhihu account posted: "Who isn't exchanging their life for money?" Deleted within minutes. Not because it was wrong within their worldview, but because it was too honest.

The frame cracked when it reached delivery drivers making $1,240 a month who'd never see an IPO. You can tell an engineer sleeping under his desk he's changing the world. You can't tell a gig worker with no healthcare the same thing.

US AI startups are now adopting these same schedules. The frame migrates wherever speed determines survival.

But China already showed what comes next. A man named Luo Huazhong posted a photo of himself in a bare room, unemployed, and coined "tang ping" (lying flat) to describe his generation's withdrawal from the growth narrative. A Baidu Tieba forum bearing the name grew from 170 to 197,000 members. The state shut it down. Xi Jinping called for "avoiding lying flat" by name.

The system punished the overwork and the rejection of overwork. Both exposed the same bargain.

Between February 2021 and March 2022, $2 trillion in market value vanished from Chinese tech. Most analysts blamed regulations. They were watching the wrong layer. What Beijing actually built was a new story about who deserves to be rich.

For forty years, China ran on a single frame from Deng Xiaoping: "Let some people get rich first." It told entrepreneurs and foreign capital that private wealth was ideologically sanctioned. Hundreds of millions built their ambitions on top of it.

But the frame was cracking from below. 600 million Chinese lived on roughly $143 a month. In April 2021, Luo Huazhong posted "Lying Flat Is Justice" on Baidu with a photo of himself in bed, jobless for two years. A tangping forum exploded from 170 to nearly 197,000 members before being shut down. A generation had decided the Deng-era bargain was broken.

The disillusionment was already there. Beijing just gave it a new operating system.

In December 2020, party leaders introduced a phrase: "disorderly expansion of capital." A linguistic category that made previously legitimate capital illegitimate. Then enforcement: a $2.8 billion fine on Alibaba. A $120 billion tutoring industry ordered to become nonprofits. Didi losing 80% of its value after listing against regulators' wishes.

In August 2021, Xi introduced "common prosperity." Crackdowns first, narrative frame after.

Six tech firms gave away $37.8 billion, over two-thirds of their 2021 profits. ByteDance's Zhang Yiming, JD.com's Richard Liu, Pinduoduo's Colin Huang stepped down from leadership. Not donations and retirements. Performances of the new frame.

Goldman Sachs built a 50-stock portfolio weighted toward manufacturing, green energy, and state-owned enterprises for the common prosperity era.

Every market runs on a story about what kind of wealth is legitimate. The investors who lost $2 trillion read every regulation correctly. They just missed that the story underneath had changed.

In 2024, some Korean parents registered to take their country's college entrance exam themselves. Not to pass. To score low, hoping to artificially inflate the grading curve for their children. This is what happens when a sorting mechanism becomes a society's entire operating system.

Every November, South Korea grounds 140+ flights, delays its stock market, and halts military drills so 18-year-olds can take the suneung in silence.

The test gates access to three universities that gate access to four conglomerates (Samsung, LG, Hyundai, SK) accounting for 40% of GDP. One exam, one path, one life.

So families act accordingly. Private education spending hit $20.2 billion in 2024, up 60% in a decade, while the student population shrank 14.5%. In Daechi-dong, a Seoul neighborhood with 950 cram schools, four-year-olds take timed English comprehension tests. Bakery sales run 35% above average because parents buy pastries between drop-offs.

Kahneman and Tversky showed that people don't evaluate choices from scratch. They evaluate against a reference frame; a mental structure that defines what counts as a gain, a loss, or a reasonable baseline. Korea's reference frame became: score well, top university, chaebol job, marry, reproduce. No family had to sit down and decide what success meant. The suneung already answered that.

Borrowed frames persist after conditions change. Korea's fertility rate hit 0.72 in 2023, the lowest on earth. Seoul spent $270 billion over 16 years trying to reverse it. The rate kept falling.

The problem was never money. One child is a competitive bet. A second child dilutes it. The sorting mechanism itself suppresses reproduction.

The question is whether Korea can replace its sorting mechanism before the sorting mechanism replaces its population.

In 2020, Jack Ma vanished from public life after one speech cost him $37 billion. In 2025, he stood in the Great Hall of the People applauding next to Xi Jinping. Most Western analysts can't explain both scenes with the same model. That's the problem.

Kahneman and Tversky coined "framing" to describe how the mental structure around a problem determines which data registers and which conclusions feel obvious. The default Western frame for national economies is binary: "free market" or "state-controlled." China fits neither, and the binary is doing most of the analytical damage.

Private firms generate 60% of GDP, 70% of innovation, 80% of urban jobs, 90% of new employment. Huawei isn't state-owned; over 100,000 employee-shareholders hold it. ByteDance is 60% owned by investors outside China.

Think franchise model. Private operators build the businesses, bear the risks, keep most profits. The franchisor (the party) doesn't run daily operations. But it retains the right to revoke the license and periodically makes an example of someone who forgets.

The core mechanism: the "golden share." A state entity takes a 1% stake with outsized governance power. ByteDance's main Chinese subsidiary has a government-appointed board member with veto rights over content. Party committees inside listed companies jumped from 6% to 37% since 2018.

The party doesn't need to own the means of production if it owns the means of interpretation.

Ma was the demonstration. His Ant Group voting rights went from 50% to 6%. Alibaba paid a $2.8 billion fine. Then he got invited back to applaud.

If you're running "state-controlled economy" as your lens, you'll overestimate coordination, underestimate entrepreneurial ambition, and stay perpetually confused when supposed government puppets announce $52 billion in AI investments.

China's "ghost cities" were photographed, mocked, and cited as proof of inevitable collapse for over a decade. Then, one by one, they filled up. Kangbashi hit 153,000 residents. Zhengdong crossed 1.3 million. Chenggong passed 725,000. The corrections never traveled as far as the original claims.

In 1993, Milton Friedman toured Shanghai's Pudong district and called it "a statist monument for a dead pharaoh on the level of the pyramids." Today Pudong's occupancy rate exceeds midtown Manhattan's. Over 5.6 million people live there.

In 2013, 60 Minutes sent Lesley Stahl to Zhengdong. She described "desolate condos uninhabited for miles and miles." Wade Shepard, an American journalist who spent two and a half years researching China's new urban districts, arrived shortly after and found the crew had filmed inside occupied buildings and called them abandoned. "60 Minutes did not find a ghost city," he wrote. "They created one."

The frame encoded something larger: a judgment about the illegitimacy of state-directed development itself.

So in Kangbashi, what finally pulled families in wasn't the grand boulevards or the MAD Architects-designed museum. It was Ordos No. 1 High School. Parents competing for catchment-area placement drove both population growth and housing prices. The frame had no category for that.

A project built ahead of demand looks identical to a project built without demand at the three-year mark. The only way to tell the difference is to understand the planning logic and the timeline. Kangbashi's planners targeted 2020. Zhengdong's architects laid out a 20-year vision. The coverage evaluated both at year three and called the game.

Friedman saw Pudong in 1993 and called it "a statist monument for a dead pharaoh on the level of the pyramids." Thirty years and 5.6 million residents later, nobody revised the frame that produced the judgment. They just stopped talking about it.
